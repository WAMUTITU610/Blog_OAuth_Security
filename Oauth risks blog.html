The Setup

Let's Talk About Digital Keys

Imagine you give a spare key to your house to a friend. Nice, trustworthy friend. Very responsible. Now imagine that friend gives a spare of your spare to someone they met on the internet who said they needed it to "water your plants." And that person? Yeah, they robbed you.

That's OAuth in 2026. And it just happened — spectacularly — to Vercel.

But before we get into finger-pointing and dramatic music, let's be very clear: this wasn't Vercel's fault alone. This is a systemic, industry-wide problem that's been quietly festering since 2017, and most organizations today are just one third-party AI chatbot away from the same nightmare.

The Incident — April 19, 2026

The Vercel Breach, Explained for non-Techie

Vercel is the platform powering millions of web apps — the people behind Next.js, serverless deployments, and the smooth CI/CD pipelines that make developers feel productive at 2am. They're trusted by some of the biggest names in tech.

On April 19, 2026, a hacker going by "ShinyHunters" showed up on BreachForums claiming to have Vercel's databases, source code, API keys, GitHub tokens, and internal access — priced at a cool $2 million.

Vercel confirmed the breach the same day. And when they traced the attack chain back to its origin, they found something almost comically mundane at the root of it.

⚠️ The Attack Chain — Simplified

A Context.ai employee downloaded what they thought were Roblox game exploit scripts. It was actually Lumma Stealer malware. The malware stole their work credentials. The attacker used those credentials to get into Context.ai's AWS systems. From there, they grabbed OAuth tokens that Context.ai held on behalf of its users — including a Vercel employee who had connected Context.ai to their Google Workspace account. That one OAuth token was the key to Vercel's kingdom.

One employee. One shady download. One authorized app. One OAuth token. That's all it took to compromise one of the most heavily used developer platforms on the internet.

"The attacker used that access to take over the employee's Vercel Google Workspace account, which enabled them to gain access to some Vercel environments and environment variables that were not marked as 'sensitive.'" — Vercel Security Bulletin, April 19, 2026

What did the attacker get? API keys, database URLs, internal tokens — anything stored in Vercel environment variables that hadn't been explicitly flagged as "sensitive" (and therefore encrypted). They also walked away with a file containing 580 Vercel employee records and screenshots of internal dashboards.

What they didn't get: Next.js source code, npm packages, or anything in encrypted "sensitive" variables. Small mercies.

The Weapon

Meet Lumma Stealer — The Malware That Started It All

Before we talk about OAuth, we need to talk about the tool that kicked off this whole chain of chaos: Lumma Stealer. Because you'll be hearing its name a lot.

☣ Lumma Stealer — Threat Profile

Born: August 2022, on Russian-speaking dark web forums.

Creator: A threat actor known as "Shamel" — yes, he literally named his malware after his own alias — who operates out of Russia. He built an entire brand around it, complete with a bird logo, a tagline ("making money with us is just as easy"), and tiered pricing like he was running a SaaS startup. Which, terrifyingly, he kind of was.

What it does: Lumma Stealer is an infostealer — it silently harvests passwords, browser cookies, session tokens, API keys, cryptocurrency wallet credentials, 2FA extension data, and remote desktop configs from any machine it infects. It then sends everything home to the attacker's servers via encrypted HTTP POST requests, disguised as normal browser traffic.

How it spreads: Fake Roblox scripts (👀 sound familiar?), pirated software, fake CAPTCHA pages, YouTube videos with malicious download links, Discord messages, phishing emails, and KMS activators for pirated Windows copies.

🎯 Where Lumma Stealer Has Been Used

Beyond Vercel/Context.ai: the Snowflake mega-breach (2024) affecting Ticketmaster, AT&T, and 163 others; ransomware campaigns by Scattered Spider (Octo Tempest); and countless smaller credential-theft operations targeting crypto users, SMBs, and enterprise employees worldwide. One log from a Lumma infection is nearly 3× larger than a log from competing infostealers — meaning it steals more, from more places, in a single hit.

The Painful Retrospective

How This Could Have Been Avoided

Here's the part where we avoid dunking on anyone, because the uncomfortable truth is that most companies — including very security-conscious ones — are vulnerable to exactly this scenario right now. But let's be honest about what the failure points were.

❌ Failure #1 — Trusting a Third-Party App Without Vetting It

A Vercel employee connected Context.ai to their Google Workspace account. Context.ai is a legitimate product. But the employee almost certainly had no visibility into what permissions they granted, how Context.ai stored those tokens, or what Context.ai's own security posture looked like. Your security is only as strong as your least-secure integration.

❌ Failure #2 — Context.ai Stored OAuth Tokens in AWS Without Sufficient Protection

When the attacker breached Context.ai's AWS environment, they found OAuth tokens sitting there accessible. Long-lived, broadly-scoped OAuth tokens stored at rest are digital gold for attackers. If they'd been encrypted, rotated frequently, or scoped more narrowly, the blast radius would have been far smaller.

❌ Failure #3 — Environment Variables Not Encrypted by Default

Vercel had a "sensitive" flag for environment variables that encrypted them at rest. Anything not flagged as sensitive? Stored in plaintext. The attacker walked away with API keys, database URLs, and internal tokens because developers hadn't checked that one box. Vercel has since changed the default to "sensitive" for all new variables. Classic case of secure-by-default arriving one breach too late.

❌ Failure #4 — Over-Permissive OAuth Scopes

When the attacker used the stolen OAuth token, Vercel's internal OAuth configurations gave it broader permissions than it should have needed. The principle of least privilege — only grant the minimum access required — was not enforced. A token meant for one thing became a pass for everything.

❌ Failure #5 — No Real-Time Monitoring of Third-Party Token Usage

The breach was discovered through investigation, not real-time alerting. If anomalous OAuth token usage (weird IP, unusual time, unexpected API calls) had triggered an alert, the window of access could have been dramatically shortened.

✅ What Would Have Stopped This

MFA-protected OAuth with short token lifetimes + regular rotation. Rigorous third-party app security reviews before authorization. Encrypted environment variables by default. Narrow OAuth scopes. Real-time token usage monitoring. Any one of these significantly reduces the impact. All of them together? The attacker hits a wall.

It's Not New — It's a Pattern

The OAuth Hall of Shame

Here's the thing: Vercel isn't special. It's the latest in a long, embarrassing line of high-profile breaches where OAuth was the attack surface. The playbook has been the same for nearly a decade. Let's do the recap.

The Original Sin
2017
The Google Docs OAuth Worm

A phishing campaign disguised as a Google Docs sharing link sent millions of people to a fake app that had literally named itself "Google Docs." Clicking Allow gave the app full Gmail and Contacts access. It then auto-sent itself to all your contacts. One million victims in under an hour. Google stopped it, but the blueprint was written.

Redirect Abuse
2018
Facebook "Login with Facebook" Redirect Exploits

Attackers chained misconfigured redirect URIs in Facebook's OAuth implementation to hijack tokens mid-flight and take over accounts on any third-party site using "Login with Facebook." The attack required no user action beyond logging in normally.

Rogue App Registration
2020
Microsoft Office 365 Consent Phishing

Attackers registered fake Azure AD apps with names like "Microsoft Teams" and "Outlook Update," then tricked employees into clicking Allow on OAuth consent prompts. No password needed. Persistent access to email, files, and contacts — granted willingly by the victims themselves.

Nation-State / Token Forgery
2022–2023
Storm-0558 — China Forges Microsoft OAuth Tokens

A China-linked espionage group stole a Microsoft signing key from a crash dump (yes, really) and used it to forge OAuth tokens that passed validation as legitimate. They accessed 25 organizations including the US State Department, US Ambassador to China, and Commerce Secretary — downloading ~60,000 State Department emails over six weeks. The really terrifying part? The stolen key was meant for consumer accounts but worked on enterprise systems too, because Microsoft hadn't enforced the separation.

No MFA = Free Access
2024
Snowflake — 165 Organizations, One Missing MFA Policy

Infostealer malware (including early Lumma campaigns) harvested credentials and session tokens from employee machines. Those tokens gave direct access to corporate Snowflake databases — because Snowflake didn't enforce MFA at the platform level. Victims included Ticketmaster (560 million records), AT&T, Santander Bank, and 162 others. One of the largest data theft campaigns in history — all from tokens, not exploits.

SaaS Supply Chain
August 2025
Salesloft/Drift — 700+ Organizations via One Chatbot

Attackers compromised Salesloft's GitHub repos, pivoted to AWS, and grabbed OAuth tokens that Salesloft's Drift chatbot held for its Salesforce integrations. Those tokens worked perfectly — no MFA challenged, no alarm raised. They ran targeted SOQL queries across 700+ organizations including Cloudflare, Zscaler, Palo Alto Networks, and PagerDuty, siphoning AWS keys, Snowflake tokens, and VPN credentials. The breach window: a stealthy 10 days.

We Literally Just Talked About This
April 2026
Vercel / Context.ai — $2M Asking Price

See above. Roblox script → Lumma Stealer → Context.ai AWS → OAuth tokens → Vercel Google Workspace → internal environment variables → BreachForums listing at $2M. The whole chain took a few months from initial infection to public disclosure.

🔑 The Core Problem

OAuth tokens completely bypass MFA. Once an attacker has your token, your second factor means nothing. Your password means nothing. Your security training means nothing. The token is the identity. And tokens — especially long-lived ones — are everywhere, in third-party apps your employees authorized on a Tuesday afternoon without a second thought.

The Action Plan

What To Do Right Now

Enough doom and gloom. Here's the practical stuff. Whether you're an individual developer or a CISO at a 10,000-person company, these steps apply to you. Some of them you can do in the next 20 minutes.

🧑‍💻 For Individuals

  • ✓
    Audit your connected apps — right now Go to your Google Account → Security → Third-party apps. Do the same in GitHub, Slack, and Microsoft. Revoke anything you don't recognize or haven't used in 90 days. This takes 10 minutes and could save your company.
  • ✓
    Never download cracked software, game exploit scripts, or "free" tools from sketchy sources Yes, even Roblox stuff. Yes, even "just to try it." Lumma Stealer doesn't care how savvy you are — it just needs you to run it once.
  • ✓
    Rotate your credentials after any suspected compromise If your work machine ever ran sketchy software, assume your tokens are burned. Rotate API keys, revoke OAuth tokens, change passwords, and alert your security team.
  • ✓
    Read OAuth consent screens before clicking Allow If an app wants permission to "read and send email," "access all files," and "manage your calendar" just to show you a dashboard — that's suspicious. Least privilege applies to apps you authorize, not just ones you build.

🏢 For Companies & Security Teams

  • ✓
    Inventory every OAuth integration in your environment You cannot protect what you cannot see. Use tools like Nudge Security, Obsidian Security, or your IdP's connected apps dashboard to get a full picture of every app every employee has authorized. For most companies, this number is shockingly large.
  • ✓
    Enforce short token lifetimes and mandatory rotation Long-lived OAuth tokens are ticking time bombs. Enforce expiry policies. Rotate tokens regularly. If a token is stolen six months from now, it should already be invalid.
  • ✓
    Apply the principle of least privilege to every OAuth scope Your chatbot doesn't need read/write access to your entire Salesforce org. Your analytics tool doesn't need to send emails. Scope every OAuth grant to the minimum permissions required. Review and tighten existing grants.
  • ✓
    Vet third-party AI tools before employees connect them The AI tool explosion means employees are connecting dozens of new apps to their work accounts every month. Establish a review process. Require security assessments for any tool that touches production credentials or sensitive data.
  • ✓
    Encrypt all secrets and credentials at rest — by default Don't make encryption opt-in. Don't leave it up to developers to remember to check a box. Default everything to encrypted. If Vercel had done this from the start, the attacker would have walked away with much less.
  • ✓
    Monitor OAuth token usage for anomalies in real time Unusual IP addresses, access outside of business hours, unexpected API calls from authorized tokens — these are signals. Build alerting around them. The Salesloft breach was only discovered after 10 days. Real-time monitoring could have cut that to minutes.
  • ✓
    Deploy endpoint protection that detects infostealer behaviour Lumma Stealer isn't magic — it has known behavioural signatures. Modern EDR tools can catch it. But only if they're installed, updated, and actually covering employee devices — including personal devices used for work.
  • ✓
    Have a token revocation and incident response playbook ready When (not if) a token is stolen, how fast can you revoke it? Do you know which tokens an employee has issued? Do you have a runbook for mass credential rotation? Build this before you need it.
💡 The Quick Wins Checklist

If you do nothing else today: (1) Check Google/GitHub/Slack connected apps and revoke old ones. (2) Check if your team uses Vercel — rotate all environment variable secrets. The Takeaway

OAuth Isn't Broken.
We're Just Using It Badly.

OAuth itself is a perfectly reasonable protocol. The problem is the ecosystem we've built around it — a sprawling, largely invisible web of third-party integrations, AI tools, chatbots, and SaaS products, all holding live tokens to your most sensitive systems, with minimal oversight and maximum trust.

The Vercel breach didn't happen because some elite hacker outsmarted a brilliant security team. It happened because one employee downloaded a bad file, one company stored tokens without enough protection, and one platform didn't encrypt secrets by default. Boring failures. Catastrophic consequences.

The good news: boring failures are fixable. Start with your OAuth audit. Do it today. Because somewhere out there, there's another Lumma Stealer log with credentials from someone in your company — and the attacker just hasn't decided what to do with it yet.
